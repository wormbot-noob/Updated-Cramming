Overview of Bradyarrhythmias

Bradyarrhythmias refer to heart rhythms that are slower than normal, generally defined as a heart rate below 60 beats per minute in adults (Sinus Bradycardia ECG Review | Learn the Heart). They can be broadly classified into two major categories: sinus node dysfunction (SND), where the sinus node fails to generate or propagate impulses properly, and atrioventricular (AV) block, where the sinus impulse is generated normally but encounters delay or blockage in the AV conduction system (2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline). These disturbances result in slow ventricular rates that may or may not produce symptoms, depending on the degree of bradycardia and the patient’s compensatory mechanisms.

Common causes and contributing factors include:

  • Physiologic or extrinsic causes: High vagal tone (e.g. in well-conditioned athletes or during sleep), medications that slow AV nodal conduction (β-blockers, non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers, digoxin, amiodarone), metabolic or endocrine causes (hypothyroidism, hypothermia, hypoglycemia), and situational causes (vasovagal reactions). These factors can lead to sinus bradycardia or AV block that may be reversible (EKGmon – Sinus Bradycardia).
  • Pathologic or intrinsic causes: Age-related degeneration (fibrosis) of the sinus node or conduction system (e.g. sick sinus syndrome, primary AV conduction disease), ischemic heart disease (especially infarction involving the sinus node artery or AV nodal artery – e.g. inferior MI can transiently cause AV block), infiltrative diseases (sarcoidosis, amyloidosis, etc.), cardiomyopathies, or congenital conditions. In sick sinus syndrome, idiopathic degenerative fibrosis of the sinus node is the most common cause (LITFL – Sick Sinus Syndrome).

Clinical presentation:

Many bradyarrhythmias are asymptomatic, especially if mild (heart rates in the 50s) or if the patient is young and fit. When the heart rate slows to the 40s or below, patients may experience fatigue, lightheadedness, exercise intolerance, or near-syncope due to decreased cardiac output (Sinus Bradycardia ECG Review). More extreme bradycardia can cause syncope (e.g. Stokes-Adams attacks in high-grade AV block) or even sudden cardiac arrest if an adequate escape rhythm fails to maintain perfusion. Symptoms often correlate with cerebral hypoperfusion (dizziness, syncope) or inability to augment cardiac output during activity (fatigue, dyspnea on exertion). It is worth noting that in sick sinus syndrome (SSS), up to 50% of patients present with syncope or presyncope (AAFP – SSS), and there may be alternating tachyarrhythmias (atrial fibrillation or flutter) and bradyarrhythmias (the “tachy-brady syndrome”).

Initial evaluation:

Any patient with bradycardia should be assessed for hemodynamic stability first. If hypotension, acute altered mental status, ischemic chest pain, or other signs of instability are present, this is a medical emergency and calls for immediate intervention (see Algorithms section below). A 12-lead ECG is crucial to identify the type of bradyarrhythmia (sinus vs AV block, and the degree of block) (GrepMed – BRADI). Further workup focuses on reversible causes: review medications (especially AV nodal blockers), laboratory tests (thyroid function for hypothyroidism, electrolytes like potassium for hyperkalemia, etc.), and cardiac monitoring for intermittent AV block or sinus pauses. Underlying conditions such as acute myocardial infarction (particularly inferior wall MI for AV node block) should be ruled out when appropriate.

Management overview:

  • Address reversible causes: Hold or reduce offending drugs, correct hypothyroidism, treat ischemia, replete electrolytes, warm if hypothermic, etc.
  • Acute management of severe bradycardia: ACLS protocols—IV atropine, transcutaneous pacing, chronotropic infusions (dopamine, epinephrine), isoproterenol, prepare for transvenous pacing.
  • Permanent pacemaker therapy: Indicated for symptomatic sinus node dysfunction, high-grade AV block (Mobitz II or third-degree), significant chronotropic incompetence (StatPearls – Pacemaker Indications).

Below, each subtype is discussed in detail with pathophysiology, ECG features, and management.

Sinus Bradycardia

Definition: Sinus bradycardia is a sinus rhythm (upright P waves in II, 1:1 AV conduction) with rate <60 bpm in adults (LearnTheHeart).

Pathophysiology

Reduced sinus node automaticity due to high vagal tone (e.g. athletes), intrinsic node disease, or extrinsic factors (hypothyroidism, hypothermia, AV-nodal drugs).

Clinical Features & Diagnosis

Often asymptomatic if >50 bpm. Symptoms include fatigue, lightheadedness, exercise intolerance, syncope when rate drops further. Diagnose by 12-lead ECG showing regular P-QRS at <60 bpm.

Management

  • Asymptomatic: Observation; remove reversible causes; reassure.
  • Symptomatic or unstable: IV atropine 0.5 mg; transcutaneous pacing; chronotropic infusions; prepare for pacing.
  • Chronic symptomatic: Permanent pacemaker if sinus node dysfunction documented.

Sick Sinus Syndrome (Sinus Node Dysfunction)

Definition: Chronic inappropriate sinus bradycardia, pauses/arrest, or brady–tachy episodes (atrial tachyarrhythmias alternating with pauses).

Pathophysiology

Intrinsic sinus node fibrosis or ischemia; often in elderly. Extrinsic factors (drugs, metabolic) may unmask it. Tachy–brady due to atrial disease.

Clinical Features

Syncope, presyncope, dizziness, fatigue, palpitations. Chronotropic incompetence on exertion.

ECG Diagnosis

  • Sinus pauses/arrest (>2–3 s).
  • Sinoatrial exit block.
  • Atrial tachyarrhythmias with long pauses after termination.

Management

  • Remove reversible causes.
  • Acute: atropine, pacing as needed.
  • Definitive: Permanent atrial or dual-chamber pacemaker for symptomatic SSS.

ECG example: LITFL – Brady–Tachy Syndrome

First-Degree AV Block

Definition: PR interval >200 ms, 1:1 conduction; all P waves followed by QRS (LITFL).

Pathophysiology & Causes

  • Increased vagal tone (athletes).
  • AV-nodal drugs (β-blockers, verapamil, digoxin).
  • Inferior MI, myocarditis (Lyme), surgery, fibrosis.

Clinical Features

Usually benign and asymptomatic. Rarely PR >300 ms causes AV dyssynchrony.

Management

No treatment if asymptomatic. Remove reversible causes. Pacemaker only if symptomatic with marked PR prolongation.

ECG example: LITFL – 1° AV Block

Mobitz I (Wenckebach) Second-Degree AV Block

Definition: Progressive PR prolongation until a P is blocked, then cycle repeats (LITFL).

Pathophysiology & Causes

AV nodal decremental conduction (high vagal tone, inferior MI, AV-nodal drugs).

Clinical Features

Usually asymptomatic; may feel skipped beats. Rarely causes syncope.

Management

  • Asymptomatic: observe, remove reversible causes.
  • Symptomatic/inferior MI: atropine; pacing rarely needed.

ECG example: LITFL – Wenckebach

Mobitz I (Wenckebach) Second-Degree AV Block

Definition: Progressive PR prolongation until a P is blocked, then cycle repeats (LITFL).

Pathophysiology & Causes

AV nodal decremental conduction (high vagal tone, inferior MI, AV-nodal drugs).

Clinical Features

Usually asymptomatic; may feel skipped beats. Rarely causes syncope.

Management

  • Asymptomatic: observe, remove reversible causes.
  • Symptomatic/inferior MI: atropine; pacing rarely needed.

ECG example: LITFL – Wenckebach

Mobitz II Second-Degree AV Block

Definition: Fixed PR intervals with intermittent dropped QRS, no progressive prolongation (LITFL).

Pathophysiology & Causes

His-Purkinje disease (anterior MI, fibrosis, Lyme, surgery, hyperkalemia).

Clinical Features

May cause syncope, unpredictable progression to complete block.

Management

  • Urgent monitoring; transcutaneous/transvenous pacing if unstable.
  • Permanent pacemaker: Class I indication for all Mobitz II (unless reversible cause).

ECG example: LITFL – Mobitz II

Third-Degree AV Block (Complete Heart Block)

Definition: No atrial impulses conduct to ventricles; AV dissociation; escape rhythm from junction or ventricle (LITFL).

Pathophysiology & Causes

  • AV nodal block (inferior MI – junctional escape, transient).
  • Infranodal block (anterior MI, fibrosis – ventricular escape, worse prognosis).
  • Other: surgery, infiltrative, toxins, hyperkalemia.

Clinical Features

Severe bradycardia, syncope (Stokes-Adams), hypotension, cannon A waves.

Management

  • Acute: immediate transcutaneous pacing; atropine/isoproterenol if temporary.
  • Permanent pacemaker: Class I indication except truly transient causes.

ECG example: EKGmon – Complete Heart Block

Junctional Escape Rhythm

Definition: AV junction takes over at 40–60 bpm when sinus or AV node fails (LITFL).

Pathophysiology

Occurs in sinus pauses/arrest or complete AV block; atria may be retrogradely depolarized.

Clinical Features & Diagnosis

Slow but regular narrow QRS rhythm; P waves absent or inverted.

Management

  • Don’t suppress; address underlying cause (pacemaker for SND or AV block).
  • Acute: atropine or isoproterenol if rate too slow, pacing as needed.

ECG example: LITFL – Junctional Escape

Diagnostic & Treatment Algorithms

  1. Assess stability & ABCs: Hypotension, shock → ACLS bradycardia protocol.
  2. Treat reversible causes: BRADI mnemonic (Beta-blockers, R hypoxia/metabolic, A ACS, D drugs/blocks, I ICP/infection).
  3. ECG diagnosis: Sinus vs 1° block vs 2° (Mobitz I vs II) vs 3° vs junctional.
  4. Acute treatment:
    • Atropine 0.5 mg IV (max 3 mg).
    • Transcutaneous pacing if atropine fails/high-grade block.
    • Dopamine/epinephrine/isoproterenol infusions.
    • Toxidromes: glucagon/high-dose insulin for β-blocker/CCB overdose.
  5. Definitive therapy: Pacemaker for symptomatic SND, Mobitz II, 3° block.
  6. Follow-up: Pacemaker checks; manage concomitant tachyarrhythmias; avoid AV-nodal drugs without pacing.

Quiz: Bradyarrhythmias Self-Assessment (10 MCQs)

1. A 22-year-old collegiate swimmer is asymptomatic with a resting heart rate of 48 bpm on routine exam. ECG shows sinus bradycardia at 48 bpm with PR interval 180 ms. Which is the most appropriate management?





Hint: Athlete’s bradycardia is usually physiologic.

Explanation: In an asymptomatic, well-conditioned athlete with sinus bradycardia, no intervention is required aside from reassurance (and occasional hydration advice). Neither atropine nor pacing is indicated. Thyroid testing or activity restriction isn’t needed unless other findings arise.

2. A 78-year-old woman with fatigue and two near-syncopal spells has sinus pauses up to 3 s on Holter, resting rate 42 bpm. She takes no AV-blocking drugs. Best next step?





Hint: Sinus pauses causing symptoms → SSS.

Explanation: Symptomatic sinus node dysfunction (SSS) with documented pauses warrants Class I permanent pacing. Theophylline is inferior, β-blockers worsen bradycardia, carotid massage is diagnostic for SVT, and “just observe” risks true syncope.

3. A 65-year-old man in ED after syncope has BP 90/60, pulse 34. ECG: P rate 80, QRS rate 34, no association, narrow QRS. Next best step?





Hint: Unstable complete heart block → pace now.

Explanation: Hemodynamically unstable third-degree block requires immediate external pacing. Atropine often fails in infranodal block; transvenous pacing comes after stabilization. β-blockers and adenosine are contraindicated.

4. A 52-year-old with Lyme disease has 2:1 AV block, narrow QRS. Carotid massage converts to Wenckebach. Diagnosis and management?





Hint: Wenckebach emerges with vagal maneuvers.

Explanation: Narrow QRS Wenckebach that worsens with carotid massage is Mobitz I at the AV node. In Lyme carditis, treat with antibiotics and monitor; pacemaker only if refractory or unstable.

5. A 80-year-old with coronary disease has asymptomatic Mobitz II (4:3 conduction), mild exertional lightheadedness. BP 130/75. Next?





Hint: Mobitz II always pacemaker.

Explanation: Second-degree Mobitz II block carries high risk of progression and syncope. Even minimal symptoms warrant Class I permanent pacing. EP study isn’t needed; isoproterenol and exercise won’t prevent sudden block.

6. A patient on diltiazem for AF now shows regular 50 bpm narrow QRS rhythm with persistent fibrillatory baseline. What happened?





Hint: “Regularized AF” = junctional escape in block.

Explanation: AV-nodal blocker (diltiazem) over-suppression led to iatrogenic complete heart block with junctional escape at 50 bpm. Flutter and torsades present differently; VT would be wide QRS.

7. On day 2 post-anterior MI, a patient develops P rate 80, QRS 30, BP 85/50. True statement?





Hint: Anterior MI block is distal and severe.

Explanation: Anterior MI often injures His-Purkinje, causing infranodal complete block unresponsive to atropine. Requires urgent transvenous pacing. It’s not benign and β-blockers worsen it.

8. While awaiting pacemaker for 6 s sinus arrest from amyloid SND, which drug bridges by increasing rate?





Hint: β-agonist increases automaticity.

Explanation: Isoproterenol infusion raises junctional/atrial rate. Adenosine and nodal blockers worsen bradycardia; eye-drop atropine is ineffective.

9. A 60-year-old with permanent AF and slow ventricular response at 40 bpm, normal digoxin/TSH. Cause?





Hint: AF with excessively slow conduction → block.

Explanation: Intrinsic AV conduction disease (e.g. Lenègre’s fibrosis) can slow ventricular response despite no drugs. VT and hyperthyroidism cause tachy; sympathetic tone raises rate.

10. Which pair matches arrhythmia to therapy?





Hint: Symptomatic SND needs pacing.

Explanation: Brady-tach syndrome with syncope is Class I for dual-chamber pacing. Mobitz I is benign if asymptomatic; complete block needs pacing, not meds alone; first-degree rarely treated; junctional escape must be supported, not suppressed.
